# Sourdough

## Starter notes
Look up starter care and it will emphasize "remove 50g, add 50g" and this is a good baseline, but I tend to fudge it to some degree by keeping a minimal level fed in the jar until I need it, then adding rather than replacing in the 3-5 days of feeding before I bake with it. In practice it works out to about removing 25% and adding 50% from starting volume.

The thing to keep in mind is that the amount needed to keep a yeast fed increases in proportion to the volume of starter. If you have 100g, you remove 50g starter and add 25g each water and flour.  If you want to feed the existing base of 100g without removing any starter, you would add 50g each water and flour to get to 200g. The next day would need 100g each water and flour to maintain the yeast colony at it's new size. 

## General Yeast & Baking
The most helpful thing to keep in mind when working with yeast (especially if you have struggled with yeast based recipes in the past) is: most bread recipes assume a counter temperature (ie; the temp of your kitchen) of 80-90 degrees Farenheight  / celsius. *laughs in pacific northwest and June temps of 67* 
An oven with the light turned on will be about this temperature, but you should put sticky notes or something on your oven controls or your yeast will die in a tragic industrial accident and you will end up with <name your yeast> III. 
Similarly, my #2 tip is get an oven thermometer (of the dial type which lives in your oven, positioned where you can read through the window). Unless your appliance is brand new, the temperature you set it to can vary *wildly* and will change the longer your oven has been on. Get to know your oven - one of mine would start at a solid 50F below the configured temp, and get to within 25F after 3 hours, but only for >400F, lower was more towards 50. It was gas and as a rule those have a smaller cycle of ~25F every 30m as the burner turns off/on, and if you are baking more delicate things, like cakes, it increases the chance of falling - cover with tinfoil tent or oven safe lid where you can.

### Basics
Typical guidance: remove 50g starter and add 50g flour + 50g water. Many bakers adapt this; the author often removes ~25% and adds ~50% to grow the starter before a bake.

Feeding operates on a volumetric scale - as a rule, you feed 1:1 flour:water by weight in grams, at least once every 24 hours when on the counter. The amount will vary by your starting volume as measured by weight- the more starter you have, the more it needs to be fed. You can keep a strict regimen of removing the amount of flour + water you feed it, but when preparing I tend to grow it a little.

### Volumetric considerations
The amount of food needed scales with starter mass. Example: if you have 100g starter, removing 50g then adding 25g water + 25g flour maintains a smaller base; adding 50g water + 50g flour to 100g starter without removing will yield ~200g total.

### Flour choices
Protein/gluten content affects activity: AP rises and falls faster; bread flour gives stronger gluten structure. Rye ferments differently (often used at 5–10% in blends); darker rye has higher protein.

### Temperature effects
Temperature is the dominant control: a counter at ~68–70°F may yield an ~18-hour rise, while ~90°F can reduce that to ~6 hours. Adjust feeding schedules accordingly.

### Fridge care
Refrigerate starter if not used daily; feed monthly if unused. Scrape off any very dark/degraded layer; discard if mold appears.

Use bannetons (proofing baskets) or a bowl lined with a heavily floured towel.

Round: deflate, fold, shape tightly into a ball, pinch seam, dust with flour, proof seam-up.

Loaf: degas slightly, roll into a tight cylinder, seal seam, dust, proof seam-up.

For fridge retard: place shaped dough seam-up into the banneton and refrigerate; when ready, warm and fold if needed before baking.


Levain: 100g starter, 200g water, 50g rye, 150g bread flour — mix and let sit until bubbly (overnight).

Approximate dough: 310g bread flour, 107g milk (or substitute water), 1 tsp salt. Adjust liquid based on flour used (AP vs bread flour).

Mix until hydrated, rest 10–20 minutes, then mix/knead until smooth and windowpane develops. Bulk ferment with 3–4 folds spaced over the rise, shortening intervals as activity speeds up.

This is the most significant influence on ingredients combined -> ready for next phase. A counter temp of 68/70 F will result in an 18 hour rise time compared to 6hrs at 90F. 
Preheat oven well (example: 475°F target) with Dutch oven inside.
When proofed (doubled or showing a slight spring back), invert dough into preheated vessel, score lid/top, mist with water, cover.
Bake covered (~31 minutes), then remove lid and brown 3–5 minutes (adjust times for loaf size and oven).

The Standard George
Buttermilk powder (or just use buttermilk in place of some liquid, if you have it on hand) is a secret to an extra flavorful loaf. ~1.5tsp

levain[100g Starter, 200g Water, 50g Rye, 150 BreadFlour]
Let sit until bubbly/frothy (overnight). If you put a spoonful in water it should float.

 310g Bread Flour, 107g Milk, 1tsp Salt
I usually sub about 30g of milk for cream. You can use water, will take a little bit less.
If only using Bread Flour: 100g liquid
If using AP flour: probably like 95g water. Its fine if it’s a little loose.

Once you combine everything, mix it good and let it stand for 10~20min until all the flour is hydrated.

Mix/knead unti smooth. You should be able to stretch a flat bit thin enough to see light through. This is mostly about being able to pinch a fingerful or two of the dough to pull it up, and spread it out, stretching it flat and thin, until you can see light through it easily - paper thin. If you can't do this, it's not ready.

Put in a bowl and let rise until double in size, then fold it over itself in the bowl a few times to punch it down.

Do this 3-4 times, it will start rising faster so the last one will be about a third the length of the first. The folding helps keep the temperature/bubble formation even (so do less if you want bigger bubbles).

If you want to let it rise overnight in the fridge, don't punch it down after the first rise and just put it in. fold it when removing, then again once it has warmed up (may need more than one fold)
By the last fold it should be airier and larger even after punching down. It will be softer and stretchier.

Prep 2nd Rise
I use those wicker basket things (battens) for the second rise. Dust one in flour real good, or use bowl+ floured thin kitchen towel. Cloth/towel needs to be heavily floured at first, the main thing is to prevent sticking.

Round:
Dump out dough, squish out air bubbles, fold a few times, and shape into a tight ball. I kinda roll around with one hand in a circle in place, this makes it into a tight little knot. Then grab it halfway around the middle and work the skin up like you are trying to close a bag around something that barely fits. Squish the edges of the bag together so your bread is a little balloon. Pinch until it stays and dust the whole thing with flour. Put it in the batten with the pinched side up. Set it somewhere to rise, it needs to double in size. 

Loaf:
Dump out dough, squish out air bubbles, fold a few times, roll out into a rectangle with the narrow end slightly shorter then the length of the bottom of the batten. Roll the rectangle up into a tight spiral tube, squishing out large air bubbles. Pinch to seal along the seamand dust the whole thing with flour. Put it in the batten with the pinched side up. Set it somewhere to rise, it needs to double in size. 

Side note - I started out doing bread in my dutch oven, if you don't already have one, they are a useful multipurpose and sturdy cookware. Make sure you can bake it with the lid on. These are excellent for trapping humidity and maintaining a stable temp/pressure, which help with the last bit of baking expansion, making fluffier bread. Gas ovens are terrible for baking due to the temperature swings as the flame turns on and off, and covered baking dishes really help alleviate that. Mist your bread and the inside of the lid with water before putting it in the oven. If baking in an open dish, put a cookie sheet on the rack under it and fill it with water. Steam keeps the crust flexible and lets it rise more. 

Oven to 475 - enough ahead of time so it is very much this temp by the time you put the bread in. Oven thermometers are your friend, the built in ones are rarely accurate.

Once the bread is doubled, and springs back a little when you poke it, put it in your dutch oven (anything you can cover helps). I usually did this by setting the oven upside down over the batten, then flipping the whole thing over quickly, and lifting the batten out, wiggling a little to center the bread. Slice the top of the loaf with a few cuts so it has room to grow, at least 1/2" deep.

Spray the whole thing with water, spray water on inside of the lid. Put the lid on and put the whole thing in the oven for 31 minutes. Remove the lid and let brown for another 3-5 min.
